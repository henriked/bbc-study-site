Climate action must come first, even if it slows the economy, scientists warn

Experts argue that delaying green policy for the sake of growth, risks far costlier damage in the decades ahead
10 hours ago
Peter Wilson
Environment and tourism correspondent
Wind turbines near coastal farmland in the UK.

Leading climate scientists and economists are calling on governments to prioritise emissions reductions over short-term economic growth. They say that the long-term costs of inaction far outweigh any near-term slowdown particularly because climate impacts are already becoming more frequent and severe across Europe.

“The benefits of strong and early action far outweigh the economic costs of not acting.” Lord Nicholas Stern, London School of Economics in the UK.

A growing body of evidence suggests that extreme weather events are already costing economies billions annually. According to the European Environment Agency, climate-related disasters in Europe alone caused an estimated €145 billion in losses over the past decade.

“If we don’t act now, we’ll pay later - not just with money, but with our future. The longer we wait, the more costly it will become to fix the damage we are already causing. This isn’t just about economics or policy; it’s about the responsibility we have toward future generations.” Leah Davies, 23, climate activist, Amsterdam.
