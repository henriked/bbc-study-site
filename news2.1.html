Economists warn that closing the door to immigrants risks stalling growth and deepening labour shortages

Leading researchers say that making it harder to enter and settle legally will cost economies far more than it saves.
10 hours ago
Jack Thompson
Political editor
Immigrants stopped at a border by police

Economists and migration researchers are sounding the alarm over tightening immigration rules. They argue that restricting legal routes to residency undermines labour markets, slows innovation, and threatens the long-term fiscal health of ageing societies.

“A large fraction of the economic growth and job growth that we're experiencing is happening because of immigration.”
Jason Furman, Harvard Kennedy School

According to a survey by the American Economic Association, only 17% of experts believed current immigration levels were too high, indicating a strong consensus in favour of openness to immigration.

“There can be no question that immigration provides a net economic benefit to advanced economies. In general, it is much better to be a fast-growing country with a vibrant, expanding population than a country with a dwindling population.”
Jason Furman, Harvard Kennedy School

With birth rates falling across Europe, demographers point out that legal migration is one of the few tools governments have to sustain workforce size and fund pension systems without raising taxes.
