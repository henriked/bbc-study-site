“Taxes on wealth are inefficient, difficult to enforce, and unlikely to meet redistributive goals”, experts say

According to experts an EU wealth tax would be a “poor substitute” for reform and risks driving capital abroad
6 hours ago
Olivia Davies
Economic editor
The European Parliament in Brussels.

Economic experts are pushing back against proposals for an EU-wide wealth tax, arguing that annual levies on net assets are burdensome, and represent a less effective way to raise revenue than reforming existing capital and inheritance taxes.

“A wealth tax would be a poor substitute for properly taxing the sources and uses of wealth.” Stuart Adam, Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS)

The IFS argument draws on evidence from European countries that have introduced and abandoned annual wealth taxes. Experts agree that such taxes have “frequently failed to meet their redistributive goals”.

“A wealth tax might sound fair in principle, but in practice it risks pushing capital and investment out of the country.” Giovanna Smith, 45, business owner, Amsterdam.

Sceptics of an EU wealth tax further argue that, without complete capital controls across the bloc, wealthy individuals and businesses would have strong incentives to shift assets to lower-tax jurisdictions.
