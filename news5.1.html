According to experts rising threats demand higher defence budgets even if welfare programmes must absorb cuts

With Russia’s war in Ukraine reshaping European security, defence analysts argue NATO members face a stark choice about where public money flows.
2 days ago
Oliver Smith
Defence Editor
The military teams at a European military base.

Leading defence analysts are arguing that NATO member states must make difficult fiscal trade-offs to fund credible military deterrence, accepting that higher defence spending will require cuts elsewhere, whether through tax rises or reductions in welfare.

“If the government is to raise defence spending to 3% of GDP, it will need to argue the case for doing so in the context of wider fiscal priorities... It would require a significant cut in the GDP percentage spent on other public services and/or other international spending commitments.” says Malcolm Chalmers, professor at the Royal United Services Institute.

Proponents of prioritising defence argue that the threats are real, and that underfunded armed forces would ultimately put civilians at greater risk.
